Old Riverman
Expects Freshet.
■—
An old riverman stated, Thursday,
that he saw many' indications of a
lreshet, the coming spring. He'pointed
out that the cold weather lias lasted
well into March and that the ice
has continued thick with no chance
for It to eat out gradually, as is the
case when a few warm days and cold
nights come early in March, reducing
the ice and snow little "by little.
The riverman pointed out that at
the present time there is also a great
deal of snow ice, which does not go
out as readily as black ice. “We. mayget
a gradual warm spell, but it seems
almost too late for that,” he added,
“and if sudden and heavy rains come
oil the top of this cold weather 1 do
not see what is to prevent a freshet
of serious proportions. Then again,
practically no ice has been cut on the
river, this season, while in former
years at least enough was cut to open
a channel, which would make a big
difference in the going out of the ice."
