plan of securing lor certain

State, and the extraordinarily
high regard which 1 lio'Id for the

is my good fortune to be in, has
aroused my inetrest in many economic
und social problems
throughout the State. What the
State has to gain by the admission
of the legalized sale of alcohol,
no student of economics can
easily assert. No appreciable
number of the enormous Hocks of
summer tourists which yearly
scatter themselves along the coast
of Maine desire a legalized liquor
saloon in their neighborhood with
its attendant disagreeable features.
It is more than likely that
the absence of saloons lias in most
instances determined the selection
of this State for a summer home.
While we may have two minds
regarding the advisability of the
drift of our youth to the large

cities, it is nevertheless a tact
that when a young man applying
for a position states that he is
"from Maine" this is a credential
of considerable value. A few
years ago it was reported that
when the German steamship
Kaiser Wilhelm der Grosse, which
at that time was the largest
steamship afloat, steamed majestically
into Southampton Harbor,
someone had painted a large
sign which hung over the bow
reading, "Made in Germany." No
one can estimate what an asset
this slogan "Made in Germany"
has been to German importations
into the United States. It is
equally true that few can realize
the fact that the "man from
Maine" inspires an unusual degree
of confidence, ami why? He is
usually an upright, honest, serious-minded,
hardworking man
who does not use alcoholic liquor
in any form and is not accustomed
to the saloon. Few employers of
labor recognize a better credential
than this. There may be
differences of opinion with regard
to the question of total abstinence
vs. moderate drinking and
■with regard to prohibition vs. license,
In business, however, there
is usually a very small amount of
sentiment and we must recognize
the fact that railroad companies,
banking houses, and other places
where positions of trust must be
occupied, the relation of the applicant
to the use, even though
moderate, of alcoholic liquor plays
a very important role in his success.
In connection with an investigation
I had occasion to carry out a
few years ago with regtyd to the
scientific aspects of moderate
drinking, I was very much surprised
to note the attitude of
banking men as a whole toward
the moderate use of alcoholic
liquors by their employees. I
came to the conclusion then that
nowadays, when competition is so
keen, when business advancement
depends so much upon true worth,
and the time-honored "pull" is no
longer effective, no young man
who has his way to make can afford
to use alcohol, even moderately.
If this is the ease with
young men striving to make their
way in an honorable business in
the large cities, how much more
must it be true and desirable in
small communities where the opportunities
are not as great, ami

one must make the best use of all
available opportunities. Considered
purely on the ground of
economies, Maine cannot afford to
admit the liquor traffic through
any channel, legal or illegal; if she
would preserve her young men to
enable them to do their best work,
her representatives, in local, in
State and in the United States
Government, must throw their influence
in favor of the enforcement
of the present prohibitory
law. The influence of a higli
state or national official upon the
public attitude toward these laws
is second oidy to that of the press.
